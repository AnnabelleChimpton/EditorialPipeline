## ID
vce-002

## Topic
AIM away messages as performance art

## Recommended format
- mini-essay

## Recommended platform(s)
- primary: newsletter essay
- secondary: Reddit post

## Why this format
- The dossier has one sharp thesis that does most of the work: AIM turned "away" into a public performance surface. A mini-essay gives enough room to land that argument, weave in 3-4 specific receipts, and draw the modern parallel without bloating into generic early-internet reminiscence.

## The hook
- AIM away messages were the first time a lot of people learned that "not available" could still be a performance.

## Angle
- This is not a post about a cute dead feature. It is about how a utility box for absence quietly trained a generation in indirect posting, audience-aware self-branding, and melodramatic status theater years before feeds made that behavior feel normal.

## Tone notes
- Slightly sardonic, culturally observant, and precise. Read it like a small piece of media criticism about teenage melodrama: affectionate toward the era, but clear-eyed about how ridiculous it was to turn being AFK into a coded social event.

## Key moments to hit
- The Daily Collegian line: "Did you see his away message? It was totally about me. I know it was." That quote makes the vagueposting dynamic instantly legible.
- The East Carolinian etiquette warning that people should never use away messages to fight or signal they were mad at someone, because that only matters if people were absolutely doing it.
- Jordan Scales's finding that AIM away messages were stored as HTML and supported rich text, which explains the hot-pink-font, black-background, overformatted emotional theater of the feature.
- Iconator as proof that supposedly personal away messages were often assembled from giant public warehouses of romance quotes, school jokes, and prefab sentiment.
- The Biz Stone/Twitter connection as the closing beat: later status culture did not appear from nowhere; it inherited something real from AIM.

## What to leave out
- Do not get lost in a broad history of AIM as a whole; the post works only if it stays tightly centered on the away message as a social form.
- Skip generic "we used to log on after school" nostalgia scene-setting unless it directly supports the argument.
- Avoid overexplaining every modern parallel (Facebook, Instagram Notes, subtweets, vaguebooking, etc.) in one pile; pick one or two and keep the comparison clean.

## Anti-slop flags
- Do not open with "In the early 2000s" or any misty "before smartphones" throat-clearing.
- Do not call away messages merely a "precursor to social media" and stop there; the interesting part is the specific performance of absence.
- Do not use flattening words like "iconic," "simpler time," or "digital diary" without tension or specificity.
- Do not write as if every away message was heartfelt self-expression; the dossier's better point is that many were theatrical, strategic, copied, or passive-aggressive.

## Estimated length
- 400-600 words
