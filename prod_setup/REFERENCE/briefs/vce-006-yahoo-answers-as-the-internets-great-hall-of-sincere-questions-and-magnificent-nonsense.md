## Topic
Yahoo Answers as the internet’s great hall of sincere questions and magnificent nonsense

## Recommended format
- hot take + receipts

## Recommended platform(s)
- primary: X/Twitter thread
- secondary: newsletter essay

## Why this format
- The dossier has one sharp thesis that can land fast — Yahoo Answers was social media wearing a search-engine costume — and it also has enough contemporaneous receipts to prove the claim beat by beat. A thread lets the hook hit hard, then stack the homepage artifact, Yahoo’s own search rhetoric, and the Greg Linden quote without turning into shapeless nostalgia mush.

## The hook
- Yahoo Answers wasn’t a failed knowledge engine. It was social media in a search-engine trench coat — a site that promised “answers from real people” and immediately filled its own front page with flirting, browser trouble, Naruto discourse, and “is masterbating awesome?”

## Angle
- The post should argue that Yahoo Answers mattered because it exposed what people actually wanted from the internet: not authoritative truth, but a giant searchable place to be confused, horny, lonely, unserious, and occasionally helped. The tension is the story: Yahoo built a utility product, and users turned it into a public stage.

## Tone notes
- Dry, amused, lightly ruthless about the product brief, but not sneering about the users. Think media-history deadpan with a little affection for how exposed and unpolished people sounded online before everything got flattened into content strategy.

## Key moments to hit
- Yahoo launched the product in 2005 as a searchable human-knowledge layer tied directly to Yahoo Search, not as a joke site.
- The archived December 2005 homepage already shows the tonal chaos: practical tech questions sitting next to sex questions, crush drama, and anime fandom in plain view.
- By May 2006, Yahoo was bragging that Answers had already surpassed 10 million answers and was being integrated into search results and Yahoo Shortcuts.
- Greg Linden’s 2007 line has to appear: people were “seeking conversations, not truth.”
- The post should pivot at least once from meme-grade nonsense to the real use case: awkward users asking about puberty, parenting, household fixes, and low-stakes personal crises they couldn’t ask elsewhere.

## What to leave out
- Detailed badge taxonomy and later help-doc mechanics unless the draft needs one quick mention of gamification.
- Overlong shutdown/2021 legacy material; the post works best if it stays in the live-product era and only gestures briefly at the afterlife.
- Product-comparison detours into Google Answers, Ask MetaFilter, or Reddit unless used in a single clean sentence.

## Anti-slop flags
- Don’t open with “In the early 2000s...” or any broad nostalgia throat-clearing.
- Don’t frame it as “the internet was so random back then”; the point is the platform design mismatch, not generic chaos.
- Don’t use “iconic,” “unhinged,” or “chaotic” as filler without attaching them to specific artifacts from the dossier.
- Don’t treat users as punchlines only; the sincerity/nonsense coexistence is the whole argument.

## Estimated length
- 8-10 tweets
