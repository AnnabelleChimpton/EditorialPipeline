## Topic
Perez Hilton and the blog-era celebrity snark economy

## Recommended format
- hot take + receipts

## Recommended platform(s)
- primary: X/Twitter thread
- secondary: newsletter essay

## Why this format
- The dossier’s strongest material is a sharp thesis backed by vivid, quotable receipts: Perez didn’t just cover celebrity cruelty, he industrialized a faster, more intimate-feeling version of it. A hot-take thread lets the argument land immediately, then stack proof points, traffic numbers, and grotesque little details without diluting the punch.

## The hook
- Perez Hilton was basically a one-man prototype for the modern engagement economy: take paparazzi photos, draw insults on them in Microsoft Paint, and turn humiliation into page views.

## Angle
- This is not just “remember Perez Hilton.” The frame is that he helped convert old tabloid nastiness into a blog-native business model where speed, personality, and cruelty were inseparable from monetization — a direct ancestor of today’s outrage-and-refresh internet.

## Tone notes
- Sardonic, controlled, slightly disgusted, but not preachy. Write like someone pointing at a grotesque museum exhibit of early internet business models: amused by the absurdity, clear-eyed about the harm, unsentimental about the era.

## Key moments to hit
- Perez openly explaining the engagement logic in the Los Angeles Times: “Every time they leave a comment, it’s a page view,” plus “I’m laughing all the way to the bank.”
- The site’s signature product design: celebrity photos covered in hand-drawn insults, nicknames, and fake bodily-fluid doodles — childish on purpose, and wildly recognizable.
- The scale jump from gossip blog to full media business: 2007-2008 traffic, high-dollar ad packages, and expansion into TV, radio, merchandise, and music ventures.
- The contrast with TMZ: Perez as the single-author chaos brand versus TMZ as the corporate/newsroom version of the same attention machine.
- The 2010 attempted cleanup, when he publicly vowed to stop the nastier photo annotations after years of backlash over bullying.

## What to leave out
- Don’t get bogged down in a full biography of Mario Lavandeira or the PageSixSixSix naming dispute beyond a quick setup if needed.
- Don’t over-explain the broader 2000s celebrity tabloid ecosystem; keep the focus on what Perez’s site specifically changed in format and tone.
- Don’t spend too much space on later redemption/rebrand material — the post works best as an anatomy of the machine at peak influence.

## Anti-slop flags
- Don’t open with “In the early 2000s, celebrity gossip was everywhere.” That could describe fifty topics and says nothing.
- Don’t call Perez “iconic,” “messy,” or “the original influencer” without immediately grounding it in the business model and annotated-photo format.
- Don’t flatten the argument into generic “cancel culture before cancel culture” language.
- Don’t romanticize the era as harmless snark; the point is that the cruelty was part of the product.

- 8-10 tweets
