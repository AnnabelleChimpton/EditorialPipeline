vce-003

## Topic
LiveJournal oversharing, fandom, and locked-post politics

## Recommended format
- mini-essay

## Recommended platform(s)
- primary: newsletter essay
- secondary: Reddit post

## Why this format
- The strongest material here is one sharp observation, not a long saga: LiveJournal made privacy feel social instead of technical. A 300-500 word mini-essay gives enough room to connect friends-locks, custom groups, and Strikethrough without flattening the topic into generic “remember this site?” nostalgia.

## The hook
- LiveJournal invented a deeply 2000s form of psychic damage: writing a 900-word emotional post and then deciding which exact tier of acquaintances was allowed to see it.

## Angle
- This is not just a post about old internet oversharing. It’s about how LiveJournal turned audience selection into culture: the flist became emotional infrastructure, friends-locks became proto-subtweets, and privacy settings became a public language of trust, exclusion, and fandom etiquette.

## Tone notes
- Dry, slightly sardonic, and observant — mock-anthropologist energy with genuine affection for how weirdly formal the social rules became. Treat the users as inventive, not pathetic; the joke is the infrastructure and the emotional bureaucracy, not “cringe internet people.”

## Key moments to hit
- Brad Fitzpatrick’s early framing of LiveJournal as a system where writing could be public, private, or restricted to certain friends — the platform baked audience choice into the core product.
- The everyday mechanics: Public, Friends-only, Private, and Custom posting, plus custom friends groups that let users create invisible tiers inside the flist.
- The etiquette layer: fandom users looking for a public “friends only” entry, comment-based access norms, and formal friending policies that made diary access feel like a velvet-rope process.
- The brutal modern parallel hiding in the old docs: LiveJournal explicitly warning that posts made public could remain indexed or cached even after being locked down.
- Strikethrough in 2007 as the moment selective-visibility culture collided with platform governance, proving that users’ careful audience management meant little if the host itself didn’t understand the community.

## What to leave out
- Don’t spend much space on generic launch/ownership chronology beyond the minimum needed to orient the reader.
- Skip broad “blogging history” throat-clearing about the early internet; it weakens the sharper point about socialized privacy.
- Avoid turning Dreamwidth migration into a second full story — mention it only as aftermath, not the centerpiece.

## Anti-slop flags
- Don’t open with “In the early 2000s...” or any soft-focus nostalgia setup.
- Don’t use empty summary words like “iconic,” “raw,” “unfiltered,” or “relatable.”
- Don’t describe LiveJournal as merely a “blogging platform” without the audience-control angle; that misses the whole point.
- Don’t frame friends-locking as identical to modern Close Friends or private Stories too early — earn the comparison after the LiveJournal specifics.

- 350-500 words
