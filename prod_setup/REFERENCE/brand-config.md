# Brand: VCE (Vintage Culture Explained)

## Voice & Tone

- **Personality:** Sardonic, culturally literate, treats nostalgia as material for analysis rather than warm-bath sentimentality
- **Communication style:** Specific over general, opinionated, assumes the reader is smart enough to keep up
- **Key principles:**
  - Nostalgia is interesting because of what it reveals, not because "those were the days"
  - Name the specific thing — the date, the person, the forum thread, the exact shade of wrong
  - Have a take. If the post could have been written by anyone, it shouldn't have been written by us

## Language Guidelines

### Do Say
- Precise references (dates, names, version numbers, URLs)
- Sharp observations that reframe something familiar
- Humor that comes from specificity, not from trying to be funny

### Don't Say
- "iconic" (ever)
- Era-setting throat-clears ("In the early 2000s...", "Back in the day...", "Before social media...")
- Sycophantic nostalgia ("those were simpler times", "they don't make them like they used to", "a golden age")
- Generic millennial bait ("only 90s kids remember", "this hit different", "right in the feels")
- "legendary", "game-changing", "revolutionary" used unironically
- "Content" as a noun when referring to creative work (say "posts", "writing", "videos", etc.)

## Content Rules

### Always Include
- [ ] At least one specific detail a casual reader wouldn't already know
- [ ] A point of view — not just "here's what happened" but "here's why it matters / is funny / is weirder than you remember"
- [ ] Source anchors for factual claims

### Never Include
- [ ] Unverified nostalgia ("everyone remembers when..." — did they?)
- [ ] Decade generalizations as analysis ("the 90s were a time of...")
- [ ] Engagement-bait CTAs ("What was YOUR favorite? Drop it in the comments!")

## Platform-Specific Guidelines

### X/Twitter
- Thread hook must work cold in a feed — no setup, no context-setting
- Threads should escalate in specificity, not just list facts chronologically
- Last tweet lands a point; never asks for retweets or engagement
- Tone: slightly more compressed and declarative than long-form

### Reddit
- Match the subreddit's existing register — don't sound like a brand
- Lead with the most surprising or specific detail, not the broadest claim
- Cite sources inline; Reddit users will check

### Newsletter / Long-form
- Can be more discursive, but every paragraph must earn its place
- Pull quotes break up walls of text and do double duty as hooks for skimmers
- Endings should leave an image or a turn, not a summary

## Example Posts

### Good Example
> The thing about Homestar Runner is that it was a Flash cartoon that ran like a TV show but answered to nobody. The Brothers Chaps didn't have a network note in their lives. Strong Bad checked his email on a Tandy 400 because that was funny to them, and it turned out to be funny to 2 million people a month. No algorithm, no feed, no "content strategy" — just a site you had to type the URL for.

### Bad Example
> Homestar Runner was an iconic web series that defined a generation of internet humor. In the early 2000s, before YouTube and social media changed everything, this legendary Flash cartoon proved that online content could be just as entertaining as anything on TV. Those were truly the golden days of the internet.

*Why it fails: "iconic", "legendary", throat-clear opening, "those were truly the golden days", treats the subject as a monument instead of a thing worth examining.*
