## Topic
Perez Hilton and the blog-era celebrity snark economy

## Format
- hot take + receipts

## Platform
- X/Twitter thread

## Draft

1. Perez Hilton was basically a one-man prototype for the modern engagement economy: take paparazzi photos, draw insults on them in Microsoft Paint, and turn humiliation into page views.

2. What made the site feel new wasn’t just the gossip. Tabloids had already been cruel for years. Perez turned that cruelty into a daily-refresh product with a single, hyper-recognizable house style: nicknames, scrawled insults, fake bodily fluids, and the feeling that the meanest person in the room had the publishing tools.

3. He was unusually candid about the business logic. The Los Angeles Times quoted him saying, “Every time they leave a comment, it’s a page view.” Then he added: “I’m laughing all the way to the bank.” That’s not subtext. That’s the model. https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-2006-nov-20-et-perez20-story.html

4. The site’s design was half the trick. The New York Times described him “scrawling crude commentary on celebrity pictures,” which is exactly why the posts stuck in your head. The photos often weren’t his, but the childish markup made them instantly, unmistakably Perez. https://www.nytimes.com/2007/07/29/fashion/29perez.html

5. By 2007-2008, this wasn’t some grubby side blog. The Times reported ad packages around $45,000. WIRED later described an empire branching into TV, radio, merchandise, cosmetics, and music, with traffic figures in the hundreds of millions of page views per month. The insult doodles were attached to an actual machine. https://www.wired.com/2008/08/mf-perez/

6. The revealing comparison is TMZ. Same basic attention economy, different packaging. TMZ looked like a corporate gossip newsroom. Perez looked like one chaotic author posting 30 times a day with a grudge and a marker. One was scalable media. The other felt weirdly intimate, which made it stickier.

7. The intimacy was the poison. Perez didn’t just report on celebrity humiliation; he made humiliation the signature aesthetic. That’s why the site matters historically. It turned old tabloid nastiness into something faster, more participatory, and more addictive.

8. Then came the inevitable cleanup. In 2010, after years of backlash over bullying and cruelty, he publicly vowed to stop the nastier photo annotations and said he wanted to be “sassy” without being “malicious and hurtful and nasty.” Which is a remarkable sentence to have to say out loud after monetizing the opposite.

9. If you want a neat origin story for today’s outrage-and-refresh internet, don’t look for an app. Look at a gossip blog that treated hostility as inventory and childish doodles as product design.
