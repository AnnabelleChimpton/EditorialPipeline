LiveJournal oversharing, fandom, and locked-post politics

## Draft

LiveJournal invented a deeply 2000s form of psychic damage: writing a 900-word emotional post and then deciding which exact tier of acquaintances was allowed to see it.

That was the real trick of the site. Not “blogging,” which is too flat a word for what people were actually doing there. Brad Fitzpatrick described LiveJournal in 2002 as a system where journal entries could be “totally public, totally private, or restricted to certain friends,” which sounds tidy until you remember what happened when human beings got hold of that menu. They turned it into social architecture.

Public, Friends-only, Private, Custom: those were not just privacy settings. They were emotional weapons, etiquette signals, and occasionally passive-aggressive stage directions. LiveJournal’s own support docs spelled out that users could sort readers into custom friends groups and even use those groups to filter the friends page. Better still, custom-filtered entries looked just like ordinary friends-only posts to the people allowed to read them. Meaning: the site quietly gave everyone the ability to create invisible tiers inside the flist.

This is why LiveJournal fandom developed such elaborate velvet-rope manners. A fandom etiquette post advised newcomers to look for a public “friends only” entry first, because locked journals often required some formal ritual before access. That ritual could be a comment, a message, or simply proving you were not a random lurker with a blank account and suspicious timing. Diary access became something between a guest list and a security clearance.

The strange genius of LiveJournal is that it made privacy feel social instead of technical. Modern platforms hide audience control behind cleaner interfaces, but LiveJournal made the act itself visible. You were never just posting. You were making a tiny declaration about trust.

And the site’s own documentation contained the buried horror that still feels modern: if you had once posted something publicly, locking it later did not mean it vanished. LiveJournal warned users that search engines and caches might still preserve the older version. So even at its most intimate, the platform was teaching a brutal lesson: privacy settings are not the same thing as privacy.

That is what made Strikethrough in 2007 such a rupture. Users had spent years carefully arranging who could see what, building fandom and friendship on top of that fragile machinery. Then platform governance barged in and reminded everyone that the final audience was always the host. LiveJournal did not just host oversharing. It turned audience selection into culture, and then proved how little control that culture really had.
