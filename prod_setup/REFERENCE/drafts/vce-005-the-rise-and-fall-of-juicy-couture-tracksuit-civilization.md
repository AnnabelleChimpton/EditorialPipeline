## Topic
The rise and fall of Juicy Couture tracksuit civilization

## Draft

Before leggings became a way to say “I just came from Pilates,” Juicy tracksuits were a way to say: I have absolutely nowhere urgent to be, and that is the flex.

That was the whole trick. Juicy did not accidentally make sweatwear fashionable. It sold leisure as luxury on purpose. In April 2003, the *New York Times* said upscale tracksuits were giving jeans “serious competition” and called Juicy the label most responsible for making them high style.

By March that same year, Juicy was hot enough that Liz Claiborne bought the brand after $47 million in annual revenue, with placement in 840 specialty stores and about 280 department stores. That is not a cute fad. That is an empire in velour.

And it worked because the suit matched the tabloids. Paris Hilton, Nicole Richie, Jennifer Lopez, Britney Spears — the whole paparazzi economy ran on looking “off duty” in a way that still photographed as status. As *The Cut* later put it, sporting a Juicy tracksuit meant “you were too good to do actual work.” Exactly. It was mall aristocracy.

Then the mood changed. The fatal detail is that Juicy pushed a “Let Them Eat Tracksuits” campaign in 2008, right as the financial crisis was making conspicuous idleness look less aspirational and more deranged.

By 2014, Juicy’s U.S. stores were closing and the brand was headed toward Kohl’s. Athleisure survived. Juicy didn’t. Once leisure had to look disciplined, the old fantasy — soft, pampered, faintly unemployed — was over.
