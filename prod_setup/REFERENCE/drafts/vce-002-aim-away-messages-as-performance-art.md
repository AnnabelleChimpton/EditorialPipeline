AIM away messages as performance art

## Draft
AIM away messages were where a lot of people first learned that “not available” could still be a performance.

That was the trick of the feature. On paper, it was a utility box: I’m out, brb, at dinner, gone for the night. In practice, it became a tiny theater for melodrama, flirtation, coded hostility, and copy-pasted self-invention. The real message was often not that someone had stepped away from the keyboard. The real message was that they wanted to be read while absent.

The Massachusetts Daily Collegian caught the whole social mechanism in one line in 2005: “Did you see his away message? It was totally about me. I know it was.” That is not software usage. That is audience interpretation. It is vagueposting before feeds, before Stories, before anybody had a polished vocabulary for indirect online performance. The away message worked because it sat in a half-public zone: personal enough to feel intimate, public enough that classmates, crushes, and random buddy-list lurkers could all inspect it and start assigning meaning.

And people absolutely did. The East Carolinian’s “AIM etiquette for dummies” warned readers never to use away messages “to fight with someone or let someone know you are mad at them.” Which is a wonderful kind of evidence, because etiquette columns only ban behavior that is already happening at scale. If nobody were staging little passive-aggressive productions in the away box, no one would have needed to write the rule.

The form itself encouraged excess. Jordan Scales’s reconstruction of AIM 5.1 notes that away messages were stored as HTML and supported rich text. That explains a lot. A plain “out” is information. A breakup lyric in hot pink font on a black background, with half the sentence bolded for emphasis, is a performance choice. The typography was part of the emotion. AIM didn’t just let people announce absence; it let them art-direct it.

Which makes Iconator, the old repository for away messages and buddy icons, even funnier. One of the supposedly most personal forms of online expression was frequently assembled from giant public warehouses of prefab sentiment: romance quotes, school jokes, faux-deep lines, sarcastic brush-offs. The performance of private feeling was often copy-paste. That doesn’t make it less revealing. It makes it more revealing. Teen online identity was already a collage of borrowed lines, platform affordances, and strategic ambiguity.

So yes, later platforms made this behavior bigger and more continuous. But they did not invent it. Biz Stone later said Twitter was inspired, to a degree, by the AIM away message, and that tracks. The away message had already figured out the core move: turn a status indicator into a social text box, then let everyone pretend they aren’t staring at it for clues.

For a supposedly minor feature, it trained people in a surprisingly durable internet habit: being unavailable, but extremely on purpose about it.
