## Topic
LiveJournal oversharing, fandom, and locked-post politics

## Verdict
- strong

## Why this topic is worth a future post
- It captures a transitional internet behavior that now feels normal but was once weirdly explicit: writing intimate things online while constantly tuning who was allowed to see them.
- LiveJournal made audience management part of everyday posting. That turned privacy into culture, etiquette, and drama rather than a hidden settings page.
- The fandom angle gives the topic real texture. “Friends-lock,” “flist,” custom groups, and later trust collapses around moderation all show how one blogging platform became a social operating system for subcultures.

## Era / date range
- 1999-2011, with cultural peak in the early-to-mid 2000s and a major trust rupture in 2007

## Core thesis
- LiveJournal mattered because it made selective visibility feel ordinary. It took diary writing, merged it with a friends feed, and gave users unusually granular controls over who could read what. That did not make the platform calmer; it made social boundaries more programmable, and therefore more emotionally charged.

## Fast summary
- LiveJournal sat in the unstable middle between private journal and public performance. Users could post publicly, friends-only, privately, or to custom-filtered groups, then watch those choices acquire social meaning. In practice, that meant oversharing thrived right alongside intense audience sorting: fandom writers locked fic to trusted readers, diarists posted “friends only” banners, and users treated the flist less like a neutral subscription mechanism than a map of intimacy, trust, and exclusion. When LiveJournal later fumbled abuse enforcement during Strikethrough in 2007, that same culture of selective visibility turned into platform-level panic. Users who had built lives around layered privacy suddenly had proof that the host itself might not understand or protect the communities living there.

## Key facts and mini timeline
- April 15, 1999: Brad Fitzpatrick launches LiveJournal as a way to keep friends updated.
- 2002: Fitzpatrick describes LiveJournal as an “online community,” “blog site/tool,” or “syndication system,” emphasizing that users can designate entries as public, private, or restricted to friends and then read everyone through a shared “friends page.”
- Early 2000s: Friends pages, userpics, comments, and entry-level security settings become core platform behaviors rather than optional extras.
- By the mid-2000s: Friends-only banners, friending policies, custom groups, and fandom communities are deeply embedded in everyday use.
- LiveJournal’s support docs formalize four core visibility settings: Public, Friends-only, Private, and Custom, with custom groups also usable to filter the friends page.
- LiveJournal later adds bulk privacy editing and explicitly warns that older public posts may remain indexed or cached even after being locked down.
- May 2007: Strikethrough suspensions hit fandom and discussion communities, triggering a crisis over censorship, safety, and whether LiveJournal’s staff understood how fandom and locked discussion actually worked.
- By the early 2010s: Many fandom users are openly cross-posting or migrating to Dreamwidth, often citing trust and control concerns alongside community drift.

## Cultural context
- LiveJournal belonged to the stretch of internet history after personal homepages but before the feed swallowed everything. People still wrote in paragraphs, but those paragraphs were now social objects.
- The platform was especially attractive to users who wanted different layers of selfhood to coexist: public persona, actual friends, fandom mutuals, and narrow inner-circle confidants. LiveJournal did not flatten those layers; it gave them buttons.
- That made it a natural home for fandom, queer subcultures, confessional writing, depressive posting, media criticism, callout drama, and long-running interpersonal feuds. The same infrastructure that made intimate writing possible also made hierarchy and exclusion impossible to ignore.

## Internet behavior / platform angle
- LiveJournal normalized the idea that every post has an audience and that the audience can be chosen per post. That sounds banal now, but it was formative.
- The site’s “friend” architecture was socially overloaded. Technically, adding someone meant syndicating their posts to your friends page. Socially, though, being on or off someone’s flist could read as affection, trust, suspicion, or rejection.
- Custom groups were the really telling feature. LiveJournal notes that custom-filtered entries look just like friends-only posts to the people allowed to read them, meaning users could quietly create tiers inside tiers. That is an extraordinarily 2000s form of passive-aggressive infrastructure.
- The site also exposed an early version of context collapse. Support documentation had to warn users that making old public posts private did not guarantee disappearance from search engines. LiveJournal taught users, a little too early, that privacy settings and actual privacy were not the same thing.

## Drama, humor, absurdity, or tension
- The absurdity is that a journaling site accidentally became a machine for exquisitely calibrated subtweets before subtweets existed. You could write one emotional screed, then decide exactly which cluster of acquaintances was allowed to witness it.
- Fandom etiquette guides treated friending as a delicate social ritual, not a casual click. One advice post warns newcomers to check for a public “friends only” entry first and notes that some authors kept fiction in custom groups that required a comment or personal message for access. That is not just privacy; that is velvet-rope diary culture.
- Friends-locked journals frequently posted formal “friending policies,” effectively turning access to personal posts into an application process. One such post explains that the journal owner excludes people they have never seen in fandom who also have brand-new or empty journals — a reminder that lurkers, sockpuppets, and random strangers were already a recognized risk.
- Strikethrough sharpened the darker side of all this. A 2007 LiveJournal archive collecting coverage quotes CNET on “thousands of LiveJournal customers” rebelling after mass censorship and Valleywag mocking fan-fiction communities as “one of the internet’s most bizarre tribes.” The combination is perfect LiveJournal tension: users building nuanced social worlds on the platform while outsiders and executives alternated between panic and contempt.

## Notable people / brands / platforms
- Brad Fitzpatrick: founder, and one of the clearest early explainers of LiveJournal as a hybrid of journaling and social syndication.
- Six Apart: corporate owner during the 2007 Strikethrough crisis, when moderation decisions damaged user trust.
- Dreamwidth: the spiritually adjacent refuge that later absorbed many fandom users who wanted more trustworthy governance and better alignment with fandom norms.
- LiveJournal fandom communities and individual authors: the actual operators of the platform’s social etiquette, from friending rules to access tiers.
- Search engines: an underrated actor here, because cached public posts undermined the fantasy that privacy edits could fully rewind earlier exposure.
