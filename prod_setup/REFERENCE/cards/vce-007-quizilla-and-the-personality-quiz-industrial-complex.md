## Topic
Quizilla and the personality-quiz industrial complex

## Verdict
- strong

## Fast summary
- Quizilla turned low-stakes personality quizzes into a full teen publishing ecosystem where identity play, crush anxiety, fandom, and social sharing all fed each other.

## Facts
- Quizilla’s founder said on the site’s 3rd birthday post that he first announced the site in LiveJournal posts, placing its start in August 2002.
- A September 2005 archived homepage said Quizilla had grown to well over a million users and a million quizzes.
- The same 2005 homepage shows Quizilla adding overtly social-profile features including friends lists, online status, public profile favorite quotes, user indexes, and quiz-list news updates.
- Quizilla’s homepage announced that users could email quiz results to friends, making quiz-taking part of a shareable social loop rather than a private activity.
- The site reported breaking one million quizzes taken in a single day on August 9, 2005.
- MTV Networks acquired Quizilla in October 2006 and described it as a top-five online destination for female teens.
- The acquisition writeup said Quizilla drew 3.1 million global unique visitors in September 2006, according to comScore Media Metrix.
- MTV’s description of the site emphasized that it had expanded beyond quizzes into fiction, nonfiction, poetry, polls, journals, photo albums, games, and horoscopes.
- ArchiveTeam later summarized Quizilla as having started in 2002, being acquired by Viacom in 2006, and eventually shutting down after a closure notice directing users to TeenNick.

## Mini timeline
- Aug. 2002: Founder says Quizilla was first announced via LiveJournal posts.
- Aug.-Sep. 2005: Archived homepage shows rapid feature expansion, one million quizzes taken in a day, and a large built-in social layer.
- Oct. 2006: MTV Networks / Viacom acquires Quizilla.
- Later era: Site becomes Quizilla/TeenNick property and eventually shuts down; ArchiveTeam preserves data.

## Cultural / platform angle
- Quizilla mattered because it let teens volunteer identity data for fun long before recommendation systems automated the job. The platform made self-sorting feel playful: Which character are you, what kind of girlfriend are you, how broken is your heart, what aesthetic do you radiate. But the more important thing is that the answers were public, repeatable, and easy to circulate. Quizilla converted inner life into lightweight content objects.
- It also sits at a useful midpoint between magazine quizzes and later feed-native identity memes. The site’s 2005 features show a drift from simple quiz hosting toward full social infrastructure: profiles, favorite quotes, friends lists, messaging/news updates, and visible audience response.

## Drama / humor / absurdity angle
- The absurdity is that endless teen self-invention got industrialized into a content mill of crush diagnostics, Hogwarts sorting, anime romance scenarios, and melodramatic “what’s your secret self” tests.
- There’s also platform creep: a site for quizzes quietly became a social network and publishing tool, which is exactly the kind of early-web genre mutation that produced a lot of niche internet weirdness.
- The humor lands in how earnestly users answered flimsy questions as if the results were spiritually binding.
