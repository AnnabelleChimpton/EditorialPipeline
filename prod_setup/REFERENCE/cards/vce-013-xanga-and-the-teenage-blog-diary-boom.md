## Topic
Xanga and the teenage blog-diary boom

## Verdict
- strong

## Fast summary
- Xanga helped normalize the public teenage diary as a mainstream internet habit: part journal, part profile, part comment theater, part low-stakes social network. Its appeal came from making self-publishing easy, social, and semi-performative long before today’s feeds.

## Facts
- Xanga began in 1999 as a site for sharing book and movie reviews before evolving into a broader blogging platform.
- A 2004 New York Times magazine feature named Xanga alongside LiveJournal and Blogger as part of the teen blog boom, noting that users could create a site within minutes with little technical knowledge.
- A 2005 New York Times report on teen online self-expression described teenagers using Xanga for poetry, pictures, music, and grief processing, reflecting how naturally teens were publishing personal material online.
- The FTC said Xanga had about 25 million registered accounts in 2005, making it one of the most popular social networking sites on the internet.
- In 2006 the FTC announced a $1 million COPPA settlement against Xanga, alleging it created 1.7 million accounts for users who had given ages under 13.
- Xanga’s internal social mechanics included subscriptions, comments, eProps, blogrings, metros, profile photos, and privacy settings for posts.
- In May 2006 Xanga rolled out Username Footprints, a built-in visitor-tracking feature that showed signed-in visitors’ usernames if they opted in, explicitly framing it as a privacy-conscious answer to third-party trackers.

## Mini timeline
- 1999: Xanga launches as a reviews site.
- 2004: Mainstream press treats Xanga as a major node in the teen blog explosion.
- 2005: Teen publishing on Xanga is visible enough to drive school and parent concern over open online diaries.
- 2006: Xanga introduces Footprints and faces a major FTC privacy settlement.

## Cultural / platform angle
- Xanga sat in the sweet spot between private diary and public performance. It gave mainstream teens a low-friction place to post melodrama, coded feelings, photos, music, and comment-bait updates without needing full web-design skills. Unlike the more fandom- and subculture-coded reputation of LiveJournal, Xanga reads as a broader teen confessional platform: more mall-internet, more crushes-and-friend-drama, more everyday oversharing. Its platform features turned writing into social routine — you posted, tracked who looked, watched subscriptions, chased comments/eProps, and managed visibility.

## Drama / humor / absurdity angle
- The funny/dark tension is that the “online diary” was never really just a diary. It was a performance space where teens could publicly narrate heartbreak and boredom, then immediately monitor who was lurking. Footprints makes that especially vivid: the platform literally productized the anxiety of “who read my feelings post?” The FTC case adds a second layer of absurdity — a site synonymous with teen oversharing was so youth-saturated it ended up hit with a record COPPA penalty.
