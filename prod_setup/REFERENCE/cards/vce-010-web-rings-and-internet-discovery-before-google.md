## Topic
Web rings and internet discovery before Google

## Verdict
- strong

## Fast summary
- Before search became dominant, web rings let site owners manually cluster related pages into themed loops, turning discovery into a social, semi-curated act. The topic lands because it captures an older web logic: wandering through niche pages by trust, taste, and shared obsession rather than by algorithmic ranking.

## Facts
- The webring concept traces back to Denis Howe’s EUROPa (Expanding Unidirectional Ring Of Pages), launched in 1994 as a looping chain of linked pages.
- Sage Weil built WebRing in 1995 by adding a centralized CGI script, which meant sites in a ring no longer had to hand-edit their links every time a member joined or moved.
- By 1997, Builder/CNET reported that WebRing had more than 455,000 registered sites across more than 23,500 rings.
- Rings were organized by theme, covering everything from fandoms and hobbies to technical interests, which made them feel closer to affinity groups than neutral indexes.
- WebRing’s pitch was partly anti-search-engine: its tagged, topic-specific rings were presented as a way to get more relevant results than broad web searches.
- Later explainers and historians consistently frame webrings as a tool for serendipitous discovery on a web where Lycos, Excite, and other early search engines were inconsistent and often bad at surfacing niche pages.

## Mini timeline
- 1994: Denis Howe creates EUROPa, an early ring of interlinked pages.
- 1995: Sage Weil launches WebRing and adapts the ring idea with CGI-based central management.
- 1997: WebRing is large enough to be profiled by Builder/CNET as a grassroots alternative to search.
- 1998-1999: The concept peaks as personal-homepage culture and services like GeoCities explode.
- Early 2000s: Search engines become better and more dominant; the practical centrality of webrings fades.

## Cultural / platform angle
- Web rings show that discovery used to be participatory and local rather than invisible and centralized. Instead of trusting an algorithm, users trusted the taste and labor of other site owners. That made browsing feel messier, slower, and more intimate: less “retrieve the answer,” more “follow the trail of people weirdly into the same thing.”

## Drama / humor / absurdity angle
- The whole mechanic is wonderfully earnest: tiny homepage owners building hand-curated traffic circles for anime shrines, fan pages, obscure hobbies, and deeply personal micro-worlds. There’s also a quietly funny status angle in WebRing being discussed as something that might “give search engines a run for their money,” which now reads like a very 1997 form of optimism.
