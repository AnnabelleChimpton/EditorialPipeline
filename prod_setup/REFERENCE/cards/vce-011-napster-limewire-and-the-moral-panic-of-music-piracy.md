## Topic
Napster, Limewire, and the moral panic of music piracy

## Verdict
- strong

## Fast summary
- Peer-to-peer music piracy was not just a tech story; it was a mass behavioral shift where millions of ordinary users treated copyright infringement as normal, even righteous, while the recording industry escalated from suing platforms to suing its own customers.

## Facts
- Napster launched in June 1999 and spread fast, reaching 150,000 users within about four months; by February 2001 it peaked at a verified 26.4 million users, with some estimates far higher.
- The RIAA sued Napster in December 1999, starting a long legal campaign against peer-to-peer services.
- Even after Napster was targeted, successor networks kept appearing: Aimster, AudioGalaxy, Kazaa, Morpheus, and LimeWire all filled the gap rather than ending the practice.
- On September 8, 2003, the RIAA filed 261 lawsuits against individual file sharers, describing this as only the first wave and threatening thousands more suits.
- EFF wrote that within five years the recording industry had filed, settled, or threatened legal action against at least 30,000 individuals, including children, grandparents, single mothers, and college professors.
- The RIAA said most defendants in the first 2003 round were allegedly sharing 1,000 songs or more, and early settlements reportedly averaged around $3,000, though statutory damages could theoretically run far higher.
- LimeWire became one of the major post-Napster successors, showing that shutting down one service did not eliminate the demand or the social norm around “free” digital music.

## Mini timeline
- 1999: Napster launches; RIAA sues Napster in December.
- 2000: Metallica and Dr. Dre become public anti-Napster antagonists, turning piracy into celebrity conflict as well as a legal fight.
- 2001-2002: Napster declines, but successor services and decentralized P2P networks spread.
- 2003: RIAA begins mass lawsuits against individual file sharers.
- 2006: RIAA sues LimeWire, extending the campaign into the next dominant P2P era.

## Cultural / platform angle
- This is a strong topic because it captures a transitional internet morality: users experienced digital files as infinitely copyable and therefore socially shareable, while the industry still treated copying as theft at industrial scale. Napster and LimeWire helped normalize search-based, on-demand music discovery before legal streaming existed, training users to expect instant access and gigantic catalogs.

## Drama / humor / absurdity angle
- The absurdity is baked in: the industry responded to mass teen and college-age behavior with lawsuits against ordinary households, while users often felt smugly ethical about stealing from labels they hated. It is both a moral panic and a status story — everybody knew someone with a giant MP3 library, a sketchy download folder, or a computer full of mislabeled songs and viruses.
