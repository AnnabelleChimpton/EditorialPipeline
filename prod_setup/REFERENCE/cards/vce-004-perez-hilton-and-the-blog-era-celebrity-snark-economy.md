## Topic
Perez Hilton and the blog-era celebrity snark economy

## Verdict
- strong

## Fast summary
- Perez Hilton turned celebrity gossip into a brutally personal, highly monetized blog format: fast posts, paparazzi photos, hand-drawn annotations, and a tone that mixed camp performance with humiliation. The site mattered not just because it was mean, but because it helped normalize a 24/7, traffic-driven gossip economy where cruelty, intimacy, and self-branding all fed each other.

## Facts
- Mario Armando Lavandeira Jr. started the blog in September 2004, first under the name PageSixSixSix.com before rebranding after a New York Post legal challenge.
- By mid-2007, The New York Times reported PerezHilton.com drew about 1.7 million unique visitors in the United States and 2.6 million worldwide per month, placing it among the top entertainment news sites tracked by comScore.
- The signature site style involved scribbling comments and crude doodles directly onto celebrity photos, making the blog feel more like a snickering school notebook than a formal media outlet.
- The blog’s business model was substantial, not hobbyist: the 2007 Times profile said single ad placements could cost thousands of dollars, with premium packages reaching roughly $45,000.
- Wired reported in 2008 that Hilton was publishing for an audience of roughly 8 million devotees, parlaying the blog into VH1 specials, radio work, a book deal, merchandise, and a music-label venture.
- His persona was inseparable from the product: both the 2007 Times and 2008 Wired pieces frame “Perez Hilton” as an alter ego built for attention, scandal, and cross-platform fame.
- In 2010, after years of criticism over bullying and insults, Hilton publicly said he would stop scrawling insults on celebrity photos and stop attacking celebrities in the same way, explicitly acknowledging the “bully” label.
- The 2010 Times media item noted that even after the cultural tide had started to turn, PerezHilton.com still drew about 2.6 million monthly unique visitors, smaller than TMZ and People but still large for an individually run gossip site.

## Mini timeline
- 2004: Lavandeira launches the blog, initially as PageSixSixSix.com.
- 2005-2007: Perez Hilton becomes a breakout gossip brand built on annotated paparazzi photos, outsized personality, and constant posting.
- 2007: The New York Times profiles him as a fast-rising Hollywood player and major blog traffic magnet.
- 2008: Wired documents his expansion from gossip blog into a broader media/merchandise empire.
- 2010: Under growing criticism about cruelty and bullying, Hilton publicly vows to end some of the site’s nastier signature behavior.

## Cultural / platform angle
- Perez Hilton sits at a hinge point between tabloid gossip and platform-native attention farming. The blog took old celebrity cruelty and made it participatory, faster, cheaper, and more intimate-looking. Its tone suggested that readers weren’t consuming polished entertainment journalism; they were hanging out with the meanest, most hyperactive friend on the internet.
- The deeper platform logic was ugly but important: posting speed, recognizability, and emotional charge mattered more than fairness. The site helped train readers for an economy where personality-driven commentary could outrun conventional reporting, and where being “a brand” meant turning your own voice into a content factory.

## Drama / humor / absurdity angle
- The whole thing had the deranged texture of a tabloid zine made by one extremely online theater kid with a marker.
- Its absurdity is that the childishness was not separate from the business success; the doodles and nicknames were the monetizable product.
- There’s also a sharp tension between camp performance and genuine harm: what read as “sassy” internet spectacle to fans often functioned as public humiliation for targets.
