## Topic
DeviantArt and the online art community hierarchy

## Verdict
- strong

## Fast summary
- DeviantArt wasn’t just a place to dump drawings online; it functioned like a full social system for artists, with built-in visibility ladders, taste-making signals, and community status markers long before Instagram flattened everything into one feed.

## Facts
- DeviantArt launched in August 2000 and positioned itself as an art-centric social network for creators and fans.
- By June 2005, Wired described it as the world’s largest online art house, with 1.5 million members and roughly 12 million works in its archive.
- At its 2005 Hollywood summit, DeviantArt said users were uploading about 30,000 images per day.
- Founder Angelo Sotira told Wired the site brought together 740 genres of art that were usually separated elsewhere, suggesting a platform built around subcultural segmentation plus shared discovery.
- Wired’s 2005 convention report described about 200 booths, live submissions on a giant screen, faux chat rooms, and users meeting offline after knowing each other only by screen name.
- TechCrunch reported in 2008 that DeviantArt had grown past 7 million members.
- The 2008 mobile web app already centered core social actions: browsing, commenting, and “collecting” work into favorites, with search and forums treated as essential follow-on features.
- DeviantArt’s own positioning emphasizes exposure, discovery, and promotion for artists, which helps explain why features, favorites, comments, and staff/community curation mattered so much socially.

## Mini timeline
- 2000: DeviantArt launches.
- 2005: First major in-person summit in Hollywood; Wired reports 1.5 million members, 12 million works, and 30,000 daily uploads.
- 2008: TechCrunch reports 7 million members and a mobile web app built around browsing, comments, favorites, and upcoming search/forums.
- Later framing: DeviantArt describes itself as a giant global artist network centered on discovery, sharing, and exposure.

## Cultural / platform angle
- DeviantArt mattered because it made artistic development public and legible. Artists weren’t just posting work; they were climbing a visible ladder of comments, favorites, features, community-group attention, and profile reputation. It trained users to think of art as both self-expression and social positioning: you made the work, but you also learned the etiquette of critique, curation, fandom niches, and being seen.

## Drama / humor / absurdity angle
- The funny tension is that DeviantArt was sincerely supportive and brutally hierarchical at the same time. It was a haven for teenagers drawing anime, goth fairies, furry OCs, pixel art, photo manipulation, and earnest original fantasy — but also a place where recognition mechanics could turn into taste politics, status anxiety, and endless discourse about whose work counted as “real” art versus cringe.
