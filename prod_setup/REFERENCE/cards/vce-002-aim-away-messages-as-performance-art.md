## Topic
AIM away messages as performance art

## Verdict
- strong

## Fast summary
- AIM away messages turned simple absence indicators into tiny public stages: diary entry, flirtation, joke delivery system, passive-aggressive subtweet, and status theater all at once.

## Facts
- AOL Instant Messenger launched in 1997 and became especially dominant among U.S. teens and college students in the late 1990s and early 2000s.
- AIM’s away message feature let users publish a visible status note to buddies instead of just appearing unavailable.
- A contemporaneous campus etiquette column described away messages as something other users actively scanned on buddy lists, pushing people to avoid the boring default and write original jokes or sayings instead.
- That same etiquette piece warned users not to use away messages to fight with someone or broadcast too much personal information, which implies the feature had already become a public social weapon as well as a utility.
- Modern reconstruction work based on AIM 5.1 found the away-message composer supported rich text styling and stored messages as HTML, meaning users could customize fonts, colors, formatting, and backgrounds rather than posting plain text.
- The feature’s cultural afterlife is strong enough that later nostalgia coverage repeatedly describes away messages as an early form of subtweeting, indirect communication, and adolescent self-fashioning.
- The New York Times framed AIM as a place where a generation learned how to socialize online, with away messages preserved in memory as part of that training ground.

## Mini timeline
- 1997: AIM launches.
- Late 1990s-early 2000s: AIM becomes a core social hub for teens and college students.
- Mid-2000s: away messages peak as coded social performance, often built from lyrics, quotes, inside jokes, and passive aggression.
- 2010s-2020s: retrospectives recast the away message as a precursor to status updates, subtweets, and curated online identity.

## Cultural / platform angle
- The key platform trick is that AIM made absence visible and editable. Instead of merely being offline, users could author their absence. That design choice created a proto-social-media surface inside a messenger: semi-public, lightweight, low-stakes enough to update constantly, but emotionally loaded enough to carry crushes, feuds, boredom, and self-mythology. It trained users to treat status text as identity performance years before Twitter bios, Facebook statuses, or Instagram notes.

## Drama / humor / absurdity angle
- The funniest part is how much emotional freight got stuffed into a tiny message box. Teens used song lyrics and vagueposting to target one specific person while maintaining plausible deniability. Even etiquette advice had to warn people not to pick fights through away messages, which is basically proof that everyone was absolutely doing that.
