MySpace profile culture and the tyranny of the Top 8

## Draft

Eight slots. That was the whole game.

MySpace's Top Friends feature asked a question that no social platform has dared to ask since: who are your favorite people, in order, where everyone can see? Not "who do you follow." Not "who's in your network." Who matters most to you, ranked one through eight, displayed on your profile like a tiny public scoreboard for human attachment.

Caitlin Flanagan, writing in The Atlantic in 2007, nailed why eight was the number that cut deepest: "It's an ingenious number, because it's just large enough to make exclusion really hurt — eight people, and there wasn't any room at all for me?"

She was right. Eight was big enough to suggest you had options. Small enough that every absence was conspicuous.

The feature created social etiquette from nothing. Does your best friend go in slot #1, or your boyfriend? If you hook up with someone at a concert on Friday, how many days do you wait before promoting them into the box? None of these questions had precedent. The widget generated them overnight, and every fourteen-year-old on the internet had to figure out the answers in real time, in front of their whole school.

And this was not a niche phenomenon. In July 2006, Hitwise reported that MySpace had overtaken Google as the most visited website in the United States. News Corp. had already paid $580 million to acquire the platform's parent company, a number that made sense only if you understood that MySpace was not just a teen hangout but the center of American internet life. The social rituals happening inside Top 8 were playing out at population scale.

What made Top 8 uniquely cruel was the absence of any buffer between intention and display. There was no algorithm to blame. If your friend dropped you from #3 to #6, that was not "the system" doing something opaque with engagement signals. That was your friend, making a choice, where you could see it. MySpace turned friendship into interface furniture and then made everyone stare at the arrangement.

Even passivity was legible. Tom Anderson, MySpace's co-founder, showed up as a default friend on every new account. If Tom was still in your Top 8 after the first week, that was its own signal: you either had not figured out the platform yet, or you did not have enough friends to replace a stranger. The feature read your social life whether you curated it or not.

The thing that gets lost in most MySpace retrospectives is that what came after was a direct response to this exposure. Facebook's friend list was flat. There was no public ranking. The interface was clean, standardized, and emotionally muted compared to MySpace's glittering, autoplay-soundtracked chaos. At the time, that blandness looked like a limitation. In hindsight, it was a mercy. Facebook understood, or stumbled into, the insight that people would pay for relief from performing their own social hierarchies in public.

Later platforms buried the math even deeper. Algorithmic feeds decide who sees what. Close-friends lists are private. The sorting still happens, but the platform absorbs the blame so the user does not have to.

Top 8 was the last time the architecture was honest about what social networks actually do: rank people. Everything since has been, in one way or another, a more comfortable way of hiding that fact from the people being ranked.
