# POST BRIEF RUBRIC

This rubric is for Lane 4 — the editorial pass that converts a deep-dive dossier into a post brief (format + platform + creative direction) without writing the actual post.

## Goal
Produce one post brief per dossier that makes the right format/platform call and gives a future copywriting pass (Lane 5) enough creative direction to produce something that doesn't read like AI content.

## Minimum acceptance bar
A post brief is acceptable only if it includes:
- A format recommendation with a clear rationale tied to the dossier's material (not a generic "this would make a great post")
- A hook that is specific to the topic — not a template phrase, not "In the early 2000s..."
- An angle that goes beyond "remember this thing" — there must be an argument, observation, or tension
- Tone notes that name a specific register, not just "engaging" or "relatable"
- 3-5 key moments pulled from the dossier with enough specificity to anchor the post
- At least 1 "what to leave out" item — editorial judgment means cutting, not just including
- At least 2 anti-slop flags specific to the topic
- Source anchors from the dossier (not new research — this lane does NO web searches)
- A single-sentence thesis stating the piece's central claim — not the topic, not the angle, but the argument. Test: could someone disagree with this sentence? If not, it's a description, not a thesis.
- A structural pattern chosen from the menu with a rationale — not just a format, but how the piece moves. If the last 3 briefs used "linear narrative arc," this one must use something different (check recent briefs in REFERENCE/briefs/ to avoid repetition).
- Source anchors that pass the quality gate: no bare domain URLs, at least 1 contemporaneous or primary source. If the dossier lacks strong sources, the brief must flag this explicitly.
- A staleness window (optional for evergreen topics; required when the piece references current-day status of companies, platforms, or people)
- Tone notes that pass the tonal variety check: if the 3 most recent briefs in `REFERENCE/briefs/` all specify the same register (e.g., all sardonic-warm), this brief must specify a different register.

## Pipeline depth decision
- **full-draft**: Default. Enough material and editorial value for a standalone piece.
- **card-only**: Topic is thin or purely contextual for a sibling. No draft produced. Card/dossier remain as source material.
- **synthesize-into:\<sibling-id\>**: Topic should be folded into a sibling's draft. Brief explains what this topic contributes.

When in doubt, choose `full-draft`.

## Format selection guidance

Choose format based on the material, not a rotation. Consider:

### Narrative long-post / newsletter essay
Best when: the topic has a clear arc (rise, peak, fall), strong characters, or a thesis that needs room to breathe.
Watch for: the dossier already has a "core thesis" and timeline that map to a story.

### Ranked list
Best when: the topic naturally breaks into discrete items, moments, or examples that can be ordered by absurdity, impact, or escalation.
Watch for: dossiers with many concrete incidents or artifacts.

### "Remember when" thread (X/Twitter)
Best when: the topic triggers instant recognition and each beat works as a standalone tweet.
Watch for: topics with high nostalgia density and multiple discrete moments.
- Tweet 1 must function as a standalone viral-format hook. Test: if someone saw only this tweet in their feed with no thread context, would they open the thread? If not, sharpen it.

### Hot take + receipts
Best when: there's a contrarian or surprising angle that can be stated sharply and then backed up.
Watch for: dossiers where the "core thesis" challenges common assumptions.

### Visual carousel (Instagram)
Best when: the topic has strong visual artifacts (screenshots, product photos, UI elements) or facts that work as slide text.
Watch for: topics where the visual culture IS the story.

### Before/after comparison
Best when: the topic illuminates how something we do now started in a weirder, more honest, or more chaotic form.
Watch for: clear modern parallels in the dossier's "future post angles."

### Mini-essay
Best when: the topic has one sharp insight that can land in 300-500 words without needing a full narrative.
Watch for: dossiers where one pull quote or one incident carries the entire argument.

### A note on length
Shorter is a legitimate creative choice. A 250-word piece that lands hard is better than an 800-word piece that repeats its thesis. Some topics (a single UI pattern, a minor platform feature, a brief cultural moment) have one sharp insight and don't need room to develop — recommend a tight word count (250-400 words). Don't pad to meet an implicit minimum.

### Comedy-first
Best when: the topic is inherently absurd and the humor IS the point, not just decoration. The piece's primary job is to be funny; the cultural insight emerges from the comedy rather than being stated.
Watch for: dossiers where the drama/humor/absurdity section is the richest part. This is not "funny writing about a serious topic" — it means the structure serves the jokes and the argument is implicit.

## Structural pattern guidance

Choose a structural pattern independently from format. The same format (e.g., newsletter essay) can use different structures. The goal is variety across the corpus, not within a single piece.

### Linear narrative arc
Rise → peak → fall (or discovery → spread → consequence). Best when: the topic has a clear chronological story with turning points.

### Cold open into a single incident
Open in the middle of one specific scene, then zoom out to context. Best when: the dossier has one killer anecdote that hooks harder than any thesis statement.

### Parallel timeline
Cut between two threads (then vs. now, two competing platforms, the public story vs. the internal story). Best when: the dossier surfaces a tension between two simultaneous narratives.

### Argument-counterargument
State the common take, then dismantle it with evidence. Best when: the topic has a "well actually" angle backed by real receipts.

### Zoom-in / zoom-out
Start with one tiny specific detail, pull back to reveal the larger cultural pattern, return to the detail at the end. Best when: the dossier has both a great micro-moment and a macro thesis.

### Montage of moments
Series of discrete vignettes or beats, loosely ordered by escalation or theme. Best when: the topic doesn't have a single arc but has many strong individual moments (threads, ranked lists, carousels often use this).

### Reverse chronology
Start with the aftermath or present-day state, work backward to the origin. Best when: the "what happened to it" angle is sharper than the origin story.

### One-scene microcosm
Spend most of the piece inside one specific incident, event, or artifact that contains the whole argument in miniature. Best when: the dossier has a single episode rich enough to carry an entire piece.

### Anti-repetition rule
Check the 3 most recent briefs in `REFERENCE/briefs/`. Do not repeat the same structural pattern unless no other pattern fits. If you must repeat, explain why in the rationale.

### Angle-frame anti-repetition rule
Check the 3 most recent briefs in `REFERENCE/briefs/`. If they all use a correction frame ("the common take is wrong, here's the real story" / "people remember X wrong"), this brief must use a different angle posture. Valid alternatives: affectionate deep-dive, genuine bewilderment, celebration of absurdity, participant testimony, comedy-first, or a frame that emerges from the material. Correction is one posture among many — it should not be the default.

## Platform selection guidance
- **X/Twitter**: high-nostalgia, discrete beats, hot takes, things people will quote-tweet with "omg YES"
- **Instagram carousel**: visual topics, aesthetic culture, things that need screenshots or slides
- **Long-form blog / newsletter**: topics with real arguments, arcs, or cultural criticism that need room
- **Reddit**: topics that invite "I remember when..." discussion, community nostalgia, shared experiences
- **TikTok script**: topics with strong before/after energy, absurd reveals, or "let me tell you about..."

## Anti-slop rules
Do not pass a brief as acceptable if:
- The hook could apply to any nostalgia topic (test: swap in a different topic — if the hook still works, it's too generic)
- The angle is just "this thing existed and was culturally significant"
- Tone notes say "engaging," "relatable," "witty," or other empty adjectives without specificity
- "What to leave out" is empty — every dossier has material that should be cut for a post
- The format recommendation has no rationale beyond "this would work well"
- Anti-slop flags are generic writing advice rather than topic-specific traps
- **Clarification**: Anti-slop rules protect against AI-generic writing. They do NOT prohibit genuine emotion. A draft that is earnest, tender, playful, or funny-first is not slop if the tone is specific and earned. The goal is to suppress formulaic warmth, not all warmth.
- The modern parallel is optional, not mandatory. If the topic's argument is self-contained in its era, don't force a bridge to the present — let the reader draw the connection. Check the last 3 briefs: if they all used a "and today's version of this is..." frame, this brief should let the historical material stand on its own.

## Source quality gate
Before finalizing source anchors, check each one:
- Reject bare domain URLs (e.g., `reuters.com`, `nytimes.com`) — must be a specific article with a path
- Flag retrospective roundup pieces (e.g., "10 things you forgot about the 2000s" from Vice/Mashable/BuzzFeed) — prefer contemporaneous or primary sources from the dossier. A retrospective is acceptable only if it contains original reporting or interviews, not just nostalgia aggregation.
- If the dossier's best sources are weak (all retrospective, no primary/contemporaneous), note this in the brief as a quality caveat so the draft writer and QA know the source ceiling.
- If all source anchors are mainstream press coverage (NYT, LAT, Wired, etc.), flag it — the draft will read as secondhand. At least 1 source anchor should be a community artifact (forum post, user-created guide, archived comment thread, farewell post), platform document, or user-generated content.

## What this lane does NOT do
- No web searches — all material comes from the existing dossier and card
- No copy generation — the brief is direction, not draft text
- No modifications to existing files (cards, dossiers, index) — this lane only creates briefs
