## Topic
MySpace profile culture and the tyranny of the Top 8

## Recommended format
- hot take + receipts

## Recommended platform(s)
- primary: X/Twitter thread
- secondary: newsletter essay

## Why this format
- The dossier has one very sharp thesis that can be stated in a single punchy line: MySpace made users do their own social ranking in public. The supporting material then breaks cleanly into receipt-sized beats — profile customization, Top 8 etiquette, contemporaneous panic, and the Facebook contrast.

## The hook
- MySpace didn’t ruin friendships because teens were dramatic. It ruined friendships because the website literally asked you to rank your friends in public.

## Angle
- This is not a generic "remember glitter profiles" nostalgia post. The frame is that MySpace was an unusually honest social network: it fused self-expression and status display, then made users perform the cruel labor of sorting human relationships by hand. Later platforms kept the social math but hid it behind feeds and algorithms.

## Tone notes
- Dry, slightly mean, culturally literate, mock-sociological. Write like someone explaining a ridiculous product decision that was also genuinely formative. The voice should sound amused by the gaudiness but clear-eyed about the design cruelty.

## Key moments to hit
- Caitlin Flanagan’s line about Top 8 being "an ingenious number" because it was just big enough to make exclusion sting.
- The profile as digital bedroom wall: glitter graphics, autoplay songs, custom HTML/CSS, and embedded media turning identity into a loud handcrafted page.
- The core observation that Top 8 was algorithm-free ranking theater: if you dropped from #2 to #6, there was no system to blame.
- The scale receipt that MySpace reportedly overtook Google as the most visited U.S. website in 2006, which explains why this social ritual became culturally legible far beyond heavy users.
- The contrast beat: Facebook’s cleaner, less visibly hierarchical design felt emotionally easier not because it was neutral, but because it hid the ranking work.

## What to leave out
- Extended corporate history beyond the News Corp. acquisition marker; it slows the post down.
- General privacy-panic framing unless it directly supports the point about public self-exposure; the stronger story is social ranking, not stranger danger.
- Retrospective "remember Tom?" fluff that turns the piece into generic MySpace nostalgia.

## Anti-slop flags
- Don’t open with "In the early 2000s" or "before social media was toxic" — both flatten the argument.
- Don’t use "iconic," "chaotic," or "core memory" as filler; this topic needs sharper language than nostalgia sludge.
- Don’t treat Top 8 as a cute quirk. The whole point is that it was productized exclusion.
- Don’t over-explain what MySpace was; assume baseline recognition and spend the space on why the feature mattered.

- 8-10 tweets
