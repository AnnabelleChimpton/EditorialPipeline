## Topic
MySpace profile culture and the tyranny of the Top 8

## Format

## Platform

## Draft
1. MySpace wrecked friendships for a straightforward reason: the website explicitly asked teenagers to rank their friends in public.

2. That’s the part people sand down into cute nostalgia. Glitter GIFs, autoplay songs, cursed HTML, sure. But the real innovation was making social hierarchy visible enough to refresh.

3. Your profile was basically a digital bedroom wall with admin privileges: custom CSS, embedded media, background chaos, a song announcing your mood before anyone read a word. Identity was hand-coded and loud.

4. Then MySpace bolted a leaderboard onto it. Top 8 turned friendship into interface furniture. If someone dropped from #2 to #6, there was no algorithm to blame, no mysterious feed logic, no “the app must be glitching.” You did that.

5. Caitlin Flanagan’s line about Top 8 still holds because it names the design trick exactly: it was “an ingenious number, because it’s just large enough to make exclusion really hurt.” Not one best friend. Not a vague network. Eight. Enough room to wound with specificity.

6. danah boyd’s 2007 writing on public friend displays treated this stuff as social identity work, not decorative fluff. That was the real MySpace lesson: interface choices were emotional statements, and everyone learned to read them that way.

7. Which is why the scale matters. Reuters reported in July 2006, citing Hitwise, that MySpace had overtaken Google as the most visited U.S. website. By mid-2006, this had become a mass ritual in the most visited website in America.

8. News Corp. buying MySpace owner Intermix for $580 million in 2005 now reads like a very expensive bet on profile clutter. But it was also a bet that status anxiety, self-display, and media attention belonged in the same product.

9. Facebook later looked emotionally easier not because it was morally superior, but because it hid the ranking labor. MySpace made you do the sorting yourself, by hand, in public, with witnesses.

10. That’s why Top 8 lingers in cultural memory more than most old features. Top 8 functioned as algorithm-free ranking theater, and for a while millions of people accepted that as a normal way to organize human feeling.
