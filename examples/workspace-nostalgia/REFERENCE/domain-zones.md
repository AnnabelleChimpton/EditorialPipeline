# VCE Domain Zones

Thematic zones the Vodka Cranberry Era brand covers. The sourcing agent reads this file each run and uses it to construct targeted search queries for underrepresented zones.

---

## 1. Mall & retail culture
The social ecosystem of malls, anchor stores, and the brands that defined teen spending. Hot Topic, Spencer's, Delia's catalogs, food courts, mall rats, the rise and fall of specific chains.

**Example angles:** Hot Topic's evolution from goth haven to pop-culture merch machine; the Delia's catalog as a fashion bible; why food courts were the third place for suburban teens; the death of Sam Goody and what replaced it.

## 2. Physical media rituals
The behaviors and culture around CDs, DVDs, VHS, magazines, mix tapes, and the transition to digital. Collecting, trading, browsing, and the tactile experience of ownership.

**Example angles:** The art of the burned CD mix; Blockbuster Friday night rituals; why people kept their CD binders in the car; magazine subscription culture and the Columbia House scam.

## 3. TV & movie phenomena
Shows, films, and media events that became cultural touchstones — not just what aired, but how people watched, discussed, and organized their lives around appointment viewing.

**Example angles:** TRL as after-school appointment TV; the cultural impact of specific reality TV seasons; how DVD box sets changed binge culture before streaming; the Y2K movie panic genre.

## 4. Fashion & aesthetic moments
Trends, brands, and style choices that defined micro-eras — from butterfly clips to Von Dutch trucker hats. The fashion cycle as identity signaling.

**Example angles:** The low-rise jeans era and its body politics; Von Dutch as the original hype brand; Abercrombie & Fitch's exclusionary cool; the emo/scene hair industrial complex.

## 5. Music scenes & industry
Genres, labels, tours, industry practices, and fan communities — not just "remember this band" but the structures and behaviors around music consumption.

**Example angles:** Warped Tour as a teenage rite of passage; the Napster trial as generational battle; how ringtones became a billion-dollar industry; the pop-punk to emo pipeline.

## 6. Internet platforms & communities
Early web culture, forums, social networks, and digital social behaviors from dial-up through early social media.

**Example angles:** AIM away messages as emotional performance; the LiveJournal-to-Tumblr migration; Neopets as a gateway to web literacy; the MySpace Top 8 as social currency.

> **Note:** This zone is already well-covered in the existing queue. Deprioritize unless the specific angle is genuinely fresh.

## 7. Gaming & virtual worlds
Console wars, PC gaming culture, MMOs, virtual hangouts, and the social rituals around gaming — LAN parties, cheat codes, gaming magazines.

**Example angles:** The PS2 as the Trojan horse DVD player; Halo LAN parties as social glue; Club Penguin and Habbo Hotel as proto-metaverses; GameFAQs walkthroughs as communal knowledge.

## 8. Teen social rituals & analog life
The offline social infrastructure of the era — passing notes, phone calls on landlines, sleepover culture, school dances, yearbook signing, and the unstructured hangout.

**Example angles:** The three-way calling betrayal; passing notes in class as proto-texting; the sleepover hierarchy; school dance politics and slow dance anxiety.

## 9. Food, drink & consumer products
The specific products, brands, and consumption rituals that marked the era — not just nostalgia bait, but the cultural meaning behind what people bought and consumed.

**Example angles:** Surge vs. Mountain Dew as a brand war; Lunchables as aspirational independence; the rise of energy drinks and X-TREME marketing; why everyone had a Nalgene.

## 10. Moral panics & cultural anxieties
The fears, controversies, and culture-war flashpoints that adults projected onto youth culture — and how young people experienced being the target.

**Example angles:** The Marilyn Manson blame cycle after Columbine; "stranger danger" online and the Dateline effect; the satanic panic's long tail into the 2000s; parental advisory stickers as badges of honor.

## 11. Advertising & media formats
The ad formats, marketing strategies, and media delivery mechanisms that shaped how culture reached young people — and how they learned to navigate commercial messaging.

**Example angles:** "Got Milk?" and the golden age of PSA parody; pop-up ads as the original digital annoyance; the infomercial as late-night ritual; product placement in teen movies.

## 12. College & young adult life
The transition experiences, institutions, and rituals of late-teen / early-twenties life — dorm culture, campus technology, early career internet, and the pre-smartphone social world.

**Example angles:** The Facebook launch as a campus-by-campus event; dorm room poster culture (Scarface, Bob Marley, Pulp Fiction); ratemyprofessors as early crowdsourcing; the away-from-home mix CD.
