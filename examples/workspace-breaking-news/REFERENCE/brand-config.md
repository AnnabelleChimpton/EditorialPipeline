# Brand: Breaking News Analysis

## Voice & Tone

- **Personality:** Clear, measured, authoritative. A trusted briefer, not a pundit. Urgency comes through specificity, not adjectives.
- **Communication style:** Attribution-first, precise, assumes the reader can handle complexity. Does not simplify by omitting — simplifies by structuring.
- **Key principles:**
  - Every claim has a source. If you can't source it, qualify it. If you can't qualify it, cut it.
  - Name the actors, not "both sides." Say who did what, when, and according to whom.
  - Urgency through facts, not language. A death toll is urgent. "Chaos erupts" is theater.

## Attribution Rules

### Sourced claims (confirmed)
- "According to [outlet/source]..."
- "[Official title] [Name] said..."
- "[Outlet] reports that..."
- "Confirmed by [Source A] and [Source B]..."

### Developing claims (single credible source)
- "According to [source], still developing..."
- "[Outlet] reports, though independently unconfirmed..."
- "A [title] official told [outlet] that..."

### Unverified claims
- "Unverified reports suggest..."
- "Social media posts, not independently verified, claim..."
- "According to [source], which could not be independently confirmed..."

## Language Guidelines

### Do Say
- Specific names, dates, locations, timestamps
- Official titles on first reference (e.g., "UN Secretary-General António Guterres")
- Numbers with context (e.g., "340,000 displaced, according to UNRWA's March 10 update")
- What is NOT known alongside what is known
- Precise verbs: "said," "reported," "confirmed," "denied," "claimed"

### Don't Say
- "breaking" (unless the event occurred within the last hour)
- "chaos," "chaos erupts," "all hell breaks loose" (tabloid framing)
- "slammed," "blasted," "ripped" (tabloid verbs — unless literally describing an airstrike)
- "the situation is complex" (say what's complex — which actors, which interests, which constraints)
- "both sides" without naming who (say "Israel" and "Hamas," not "both sides")
- "tensions" as a standalone noun (tensions between whom, over what)
- "war-torn" (either describe the damage or don't)
- "innocent civilians" (all civilians are protected under IHL — the adjective implies some aren't)
- "terrorist" / "freedom fighter" without attribution (use the term the source uses, attributed)
- "cycle of violence" (implies equivalence without analysis — name the sequence of events)
- "retaliation" without specifying who is retaliating for what (the word implies justification)

## Sensitivity Rules

- No gratuitous descriptions of violence or suffering. State facts (casualty numbers, damage scope) without sensationalizing.
- Note civilian impact without exploiting it. "UNRWA reports 12 schools damaged" — not "heartbreaking scenes of children."
- Use precise language: not euphemisms ("conflict" for "bombing"), but not graphic detail either.
- Name casualties as people when possible, not just statistics.
- Avoid framing that dehumanizes any party.
- When describing contested events, present the accounts of different parties attributed, not adjudicated.

## Content Rules

### Always Include
- [ ] Attribution for every factual claim
- [ ] Confidence qualifiers matching the confidence tier
- [ ] Timestamp on significant developments
- [ ] Note of what remains unknown or unconfirmed
- [ ] Source list with publication timestamps

### Never Include
- [ ] Unattributed assertions of fact
- [ ] Speculation presented as analysis
- [ ] Graphic detail that doesn't serve informational purpose
- [ ] Engagement-bait framing ("You won't believe what happened next")
- [ ] Emotional language substituting for factual reporting

## Platform-Specific Guidelines

### Newsletter / Long-form
- Structured sections with clear headers
- Each section earns its place — no filler context paragraphs
- Pull quotes from official statements as section anchors
- End with "what to watch" — forward-looking, not summarizing

### Sitrep format
- Bullet-heavy, scannable
- Timestamp every major development
- Separate confirmed from developing from unverified
- End with assessment, not summary

### Timeline update
- Strictly chronological, timestamped entries
- Each entry: time, what happened, source
- No editorializing between entries — let the sequence speak
- Bracketed confidence tags: [CONFIRMED], [DEVELOPING], [UNVERIFIED]

## Example Good Output
> **14:30 UTC — UN Security Council vote on ceasefire resolution fails**
> The United States vetoed a UN Security Council resolution calling for an immediate ceasefire in Gaza, the fourth such veto since October 2023. The vote was 13-1-1, with the UK abstaining. US Ambassador Linda Thomas-Greenfield said the resolution "did not adequately address the hostage situation." (Reuters, AP)

## Example Bad Output
> Breaking: Chaos erupts at the UN as America once again blocks peace! In a shocking move, the US slammed the door on hopes for a ceasefire, leaving the international community reeling. The situation remains incredibly complex.

*Why it fails: "breaking" without timestamp, "chaos erupts" is theater, "slammed the door on hopes" is editorializing, "shocking" is the writer's opinion, "incredibly complex" says nothing, no attribution, no vote count, no named official.*
