## ID
vce-001

## Topic
MySpace profile culture and the tyranny of the Top 8

## Recommended format
- narrative long-post

## Recommended platform(s)
- primary: newsletter essay
- secondary: Reddit post (r/nostalgia or r/internetculture — the discussion-bait is built in)

## Why this format
- The dossier has a genuine thesis (interface design manufacturing social pain), a clean arc (launch, peak saturation, Facebook as relief), and the kind of specific evidence that rewards room to breathe. This is not a list or a hot take; it is a story about how a widget rewired friendships.

## The hook
- MySpace did not just let you rank your friends. It made you do it, in public, with a number — and eight turned out to be the cruelest possible choice.

## Angle
- Top 8 was the last time a social platform forced users to perform their own social math without an algorithm to absorb the blame. The design cruelty was the feature. Everything that came after — Facebook's flat friend lists, algorithmic feeds, private stories — was partly a retreat from what MySpace proved people would do to each other when given a public ranking widget.

## Tone notes
- Sardonic but not sneering. The writer remembers this era and finds it both funny and a little bit horrifying in retrospect. Think: a smart friend telling you a story at a bar, not a cultural critic delivering a thesis. Allow some warmth toward the absurdity without letting it curdle into "weren't we all so silly." The underlying seriousness is real: product design shaped real social pain.

## Key moments to hit
1. The Flanagan quote: "It's an ingenious number, because it's just large enough to make exclusion really hurt." This is the structural anchor.
2. The ritual logic of maintenance: best friend or boyfriend in slot #1? How fast can you promote someone after a hookup? The etiquette that emerged around a feature that had none built in.
3. Tom Anderson as the default friend — the co-founder who occupied a Top 8 slot if you did nothing, which meant even passivity was a social signal.
4. The Facebook contrast: blandness as relief. Facebook's less hierarchical friend display was not a design limitation; it was an emotional mercy.
5. The $580 million News Corp. acquisition as a scale marker — this was not a niche teen thing; it was the most visited website in America.

## What to leave out
- The HTML/CSS customization angle. It is interesting and the dossier covers it well, but it is a separate post topic (profile-as-bedroom-wall). Including it dilutes the Top 8 argument. Mention profile culture in passing for atmosphere, but do not give it structural weight.
- The danah boyd scholarly framing. Her work is a source, not a narrative beat. Cite her if needed, but do not turn the post into a media-studies summary.
- The open questions about when Top 8 expanded beyond 8. Interesting research gap, but the post should treat "Top 8" as the cultural object it became, not litigate the product changelog.

## Anti-slop flags
- Do not open with "In the early 2000s..." or "Back when MySpace was king..." or any era-establishing throat-clear. Start with the feature, not the decade.
- Do not use the word "iconic." MySpace nostalgia content overuses it to the point of meaninglessness.
- Do not frame this as "simpler times" nostalgia. The whole point is that it was not simple at all — it was socially brutal in a way current platforms obscure.
- Do not stack "remember when" prompts. One is fine for texture. Three in a row is a listicle in disguise.
- Avoid "digital" as a modifier for things that are obviously digital. "Digital friendships," "digital identities" — the reader knows it is online.

## Estimated length
- 600-750 words
